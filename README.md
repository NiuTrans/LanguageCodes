# Language Codes

It is hard to tell the exact number of human languages on this planet because the definition of "language" varies depending on how one defines the distinction between languages and dialects. E.g., some languages can be grouped into a language family, and show small differences from others. For a general definition of language, the number of living languages is over 7,000<sup><a id="4">[[1]](#1)</a></sup> but most of them are non-digitized. Here we list 353 languages with their codes, families, regions and etc. This list covers most of the majority languages in the world and a large number of minority languages. Also, we collect links to sites of multi-lingual corpora. They might help when one studies these languages and/or develop multi-lingual natural language processing (NLP) systems.

- ### Multi-lingual Data Shared by MT Conference：

  [CCMT](http://mteval.cipsc.org.cn:81/CCMT2021/index.html)	China Conference on Machine Translation (CCMT) , formerly known as China Workshop on Machine Translation (CWMT), is an annual conference starting from 2005. It is the flagship conference of machine translation in China. CCMT has organized multiple evaluation which has profoundly influenced domestic machine translation technology. Its evaluations focus mainly on Chinese, English and domestic minority languages (Mongolian, Tibetan, Uyghur, etc.) in domains of news, spoken languages, governmental documents, etc. Automatic evaluation is mainly used. The metrics for automatic evaluation generally include BLEU-SBP, BLEU-NIST, TER, METEOR, NIST, GTM, mWER, mPER, and ICT. The conference attracts nearly dozens of companies and scientific research institutions domestic and overseas every year.  For more information about CCMT, please refer to the relevant page of the Technical Committee on Machine Translation, Chinese Information Processing Society of China.

   [WMT](http://www.statmt.org/wmt21/)	WMT is hosted by Special Interest Group for Machine Translation (SIGMT) annually since 2006. It is a comprehensive conference involving multiple tasks of machine translation, including multi-domain translation evaluation task, quality estimation task and other machine translation related tasks (such as document alignment, etc.). Now WMT has become a flagship evaluation conference in machine translation, and many researches use WM evaluation results as benchmarks. WMT evaluation involves a wide range of languages, including more than ten languages such as English, German, Finnish, Czech, Romanian, etc. The translation direction is generally based on English to explore the translation performance between English and other languages, in domains of news, information technology, biomedicine. WMT is similar to CCMT in terms of evaluation. It also uses a combination of manual and automatic evaluation. The automatic evaluation metrics are generally BLEU, TER, etc. **In addition, WMT publishes all evaluation data on line for free.** For more information about WMT machine translation evaluation, please refer to SIGMT official website.

   [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/itl/iad/mig/open-machine-translation-evaluation)	The NIST machine translation evaluation started in 2001 as part of the DARPA TIDES program. The evaluations are driven and coordinated by NIST as NIST OpenMT. The settings of many tasks in WMT and CCMT now also refer to the content of NIST evaluations. In the early days, NIST evaluations mainly evaluated the translation performance from languages such as Arabic and Chinese to English, generally using a combination of manual and automatic evaluation. For human evaluation, a 5-point scale system is adopted. For automatic evaluation, there are many metrics, including BLEU, METEOR, TER and HyTER. Furthermore, NIST has begun to evaluate low-resource language technologies since 2016. **At the same time, the datasets provided by NIST is popular among many scientific researchers for its high quality.** For example, parallel corpora such as MT04 and MT06 (Chinese-English) are often used by researchers in experiments.** Results of past NIST OpenMT and DARPA TIDES MT evaluations as well as resources specific to each evaluation can be accessed via the year-specific links. However, NIST evaluations have ceased in recent years. For more information about NIST evaluations, please refer to the official website.


   [IWSLT](https://iwslt.org/)	IWSLT. The International Conference on Spoken Language Translation (IWSLT), which has been held annually since 2004, is also a distinctive evaluation campaign on spoken language translation. The test data includes multilingual subtitles of TED talks and QED lectures. The languages involve English, French, German, Czech, Chinese, Arabic and many other languages. In addition, IWSLT 2016 also added a task for daily conversations translation--to translate a conversation in Microsoft Skype from one language into other languages. Similar to WMT, automatic evaluation is used in IWSLT, generally BLEU and other metrics. Besides the evaluation of text-to-text translation, IWSLT also has evaluations of automatic speech recognition and speech-to-text into another language. For more information about IWSLT, please refer to the the official website.

  
  [WAT](http://lotus.kuee.kyoto-u.ac.jp/WAT/)  The Workshop on Asian Translation (WAT) is a new open evaluation campaign focusing on Asian languages. It is jointly organized by the Japan Science and Technology Agency (JST), the National Institute of Information and Communications Technology (NICT) and other institutions, aiming to provide easier communication and integration among Asian countries. WAT focuses on translation from mainstream Asian languages (Chinese, Korean, Hindi, etc.) and English to Japanese in comprehensive domains such as academic papers, patents, news and recipes. Evaluation metrics include automatic evaluation (BLEU, RIBES, AMFM, etc.) and manual evaluation. It’s feature is that the tests are  evaluated in units of paragraphs to examine the translation performance in context. For more information about WAT, please refer to the official website.

  [NTCIR](http://research.nii.ac.jp/ntcir/ntcir-16/index.html)	The NACSIS Test Collections for IR (NTCIR) is organized by the National Center for Science Information Systems of Japan, aiming to establish a Japanese standard testbed for natural language processing and information retrieval-related tasks. The Patent Machine Translation (PatentMT) task added in NTCIR-9 and NTCIR-10 mainly focused on patent translation evaluation, in order to promote the development and application of machine translation in the field of patents. A combination of manual and automatic evaluation was adopted in NTCIR-9, with manual evaluation as the leading factor. Manual evaluation is mainly based on the accuracy and fluency, while for automatic evaluation the metrics such as BLEU, NIST are used. For more information about NTCIR evaluation, please refer to the official website.

   ##### The above MT evaluation-related data including development, training and test data can be downloaded from their websites. However, for some data you can also submit your application on [LDC](https://catalog.ldc.upenn.edu/byyear).  From the perspective of machine translation development, these evaluation tasks provide benchmark datasets for related research, so that different systems can be compared and analyzed under the same conditions, thereby establishing the experimental basis for machine translation research. In addition, the open evaluation also enables researchers to learn about the latest results of machine translation research at the first time.
